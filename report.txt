Topic 5: Primary Physical Forcings (PBLH & Temperature)
In atmospheric science, PM2.5 concentrations are not just a result of how much "smoke" is released, but how much "room" that smoke has to move. Your EDA identifies Planetary Boundary Layer Height (PBLH) and Temperature (t2) as the two most powerful physical levers in the dataset.

1. PBLH: The Atmospheric "Lid"
The Planetary Boundary Layer Height is the single strongest predictor in your correlation analysis (r=−0.749).

The Inverse Law: There is a near-perfect inverse relationship between PBLH and PM2.5.

Low PBLH (Night/Winter): The "lid" on the atmosphere drops (often below 500m). Pollutants are trapped in a very small volume of air near the ground, leading to massive concentration spikes.

High PBLH (Day/Summer): Solar heating causes the air to mix vertically, raising the lid (up to 3000m+). This dilutes the particles across a much larger volume, lowering the concentration even if emissions remain constant.

EDA Discovery (Section 19): During "Normal Hours," the mean PBLH is ~768m. During "Extreme Pollution Events," the mean PBLH drops to 384m. This confirms that extreme events are physically driven by atmospheric stagnation.

2. Temperature (t2): The Stability Driver
Temperature is the second most dominant feature (r=−0.724). Its role is tied to the physics of Temperature Inversions.

Cold Air Trapping: Cold surface temperatures (common in December) lead to stable atmospheric conditions where cold, heavy air stays near the ground, pinned down by warmer air above. This "inversion" prevents vertical mixing.

The Winter Signal: Your EDA shows that the mean temperature during extreme episodes is only 10.2°C, compared to the normal mean of 18.5°C.

Phase Shift: While temperature is a great predictor for the first 10 hours, its "future" predictability at 16 hours is "Moderate" (r≈0.59). The model must account for the fact that temperature follows a solar cycle, which "resets" the mixing potential every morning.

3. Spatial Non-Stationarity (Section 20)

Indo-Gangetic Plain (IGP): Shows a strong negative correlation with temperature. This is where the winter inversion is most lethal.

Southern India: Shows a weak correlation. In coastal areas, sea breezes and higher humidity matter more than the temperature-driven "lid" effect.

Feature Tiering: Your plan to put pblh and t2 in Tier 1 is physically correct. These provide the "structural context" for the air.

Non-Linearity: The relationship between PBLH and PM2.5 is often non-linear (hyperbolic). A model like a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) is better at learning these global physical operators than a simple linear regressor.

Topic 8: Spatio-Temporal Rhythms (Diurnal Cycles & Autocorrelation)
The atmosphere is not a static picture; it is a breathing, rhythmic system. Your EDA identifies two primary temporal signals that dictate predictability: the 24-hour Diurnal Heartbeat and the Autocorrelation Decay over time.

Every single season in the dataset (April, July, October, December) exhibits a powerful cyclic pattern tied to the solar day.

The Physical Cycle: As the sun rises, the ground heats up, causing the Planetary Boundary Layer (PBLH) to expand upward. This dilutes PM2.5, causing a valley in concentration during the afternoon. As the sun sets, the layer collapses, trapping fresh emissions in a shallow "nighttime lid," leading to a sharp peak.

Seasonal Amplitude: Your EDA (Section 6) shows that this "heartbeat" is much louder in winter. In December, the difference between the nighttime peak and the daytime valley is nearly 3x, whereas in July (Monsoon), cloud cover and rain flatten this cycle significantly.

Near-Infinite Persistence (t=1 to t=4): Correlation stays above 0.90. This means for the first few hours, the atmosphere barely changes. A model that doesn't "know" this will likely produce flickering or noisy predictions that violate physics.

The 10-Hour "Cliff" (t=10): This is where your target input ends. Correlation is still relatively strong (~0.65 to 0.75).

The 24-Hour "Echo": You'll notice a distinct "bump" in the correlation plots at the 24-hour mark. This confirms that the state of the atmosphere at 9:00 PM tonight is highly predictive of 9:00 PM tomorrow.

The first 2–3 hours provide the "momentum" of the plume.
