### Topic 1: The Tucker-Factorized Spectral Layer

Your EDA showed that we only have **2,932 training hours** but a grid of **17,360 pixels**. A standard FNO is a "parameter monster"—a single spectral layer with 64 channels and 20 modes has over **4 million complex parameters**. On your small dataset, this model would simply memorize the noise of 2016 and fail on the 2017 test set.

The **Tucker-FNO (TFNO)** is your solution for mathematical regularization.

#### 1. The Low-Rank Tensor Hypothesis

Your PCA (Topic 29) proved that **4 modes explain 70% of the variance**. This confirms that the atmosphere has a "Low-Rank" structure. The Tucker-Factorized layer exploits this by decomposing the massive 4D spectral weight tensor $W \in \mathbb{C}^{C_{in} \times C_{out} \times M \times M}$ into:

* **One small core tensor ($G$):** This represents the shared physical interactions.
* **Four factor matrices ($U_1, U_2, U_3, U_4$):** These project the channels and frequencies into a compressed latent space.

#### 2. Why This Wins the Hackathon

* **Parameter Efficiency:** It reduces the number of parameters in the spectral block by **60–75%**.
* **Implicit Regularization:** By forcing the weights to be low-rank, you prevent the model from learning "high-frequency noise" (pixel-level artifacts) and force it to focus on the "global transport" (the IGP-scale dynamics).
* **Memory Headroom:** Because the layers are smaller, you can use a **larger width (96 or 128 channels)** on the same P100 GPU, allowing the model to "think" more deeply about the 16 different features without running out of VRAM.

### Topic 2: Fractional Fourier Transform (FrFT) Logic

Standard FNOs use the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). FFT assumes that a signal's frequency doesn't change over time (stationarity). However, your EDA (Topic 8 and 28) showed that the **diurnal cycle is non-stationary**—the physics of $t=0$ (midnight) is rotated compared to $t=12$ (noon).

#### 1. The Learnable Rotation ($\alpha$)

The **Fractional Fourier Transform (FrFT)** is a generalization of the FFT. Think of it as rotating the signal in the "time-frequency plane" by an angle $\alpha$.

* At $\alpha = \pi/2$, it is a standard FFT.
* At $\alpha = 0$, it is the identity (time domain).
* **The Innovation:** We make $\alpha$ a **learnable parameter** in your spectral layers.

#### 2. Handling the Diurnal "Phase Flip"

Your EDA showed that the correlation of `pblh` flips from negative to positive as the day progresses.

* **Standard FNO:** Struggles to capture this "switch" because it treats the whole 26-hour window with one frequency filter.
* **FrFT:** By "rotating" the signal, the model can adapt its frequency response to match the specific phase of the diurnal cycle. It allows the model to "see" the spectral information of the atmosphere in a way that aligns with the sun's position. This is the "secret weapon" used in the **CoNOAir** paper by the IIT Delhi organizers.

### Topic 3: The Dual-Branch Information Pipeline

You have two types of information with different temporal "confidence":

1. **State Memory:** The 10 hours of `cpm25` (High confidence, but short).
2. **Future Forcing:** The 26 hours of Meteorology (Lower confidence/simulated, but long).

#### 1. The Specialized Encoders

* **The Concentration Branch:** Processes only the first 10 hours of PM2.5. It uses a **Swin3D encoder** with a small window to "remember" where the plumes are right now.
* **The Forcing Branch:** Processes the full 26 hours of weather. It uses a **ConvLSTM** or a **Linear Transformer** to understand the "evolutionary pressure" the wind and PBLH will exert on those plumes.

#### 2. Cross-Attention Fusion

Instead of just concatenating them, we use **Cross-Attention**.

* **Query ($Q$):** The 10h PM2.5 state.
* **Key/Value ($K, V$):** The 26h meteorological forcing.
* **The Result:** The model "asks" the weather data: *"Based on the plumes I see at $t=9$, which wind vectors in the 26h forecast should I pay attention to?"* This ensures the model doesn't just treat the weather as background noise but uses it to "steer" the initial concentration.

#### 1. The Swin3D + FNO Hybrid

#### 2. Why This Wins

### Topic 5: Horizon-Specialized Output Heads

Your EDA Section 25 showed that the **Persistence Baseline** is extremely hard to beat in the first few hours but plateaus at the end. This means the "features" the model needs for Hour 1 are completely different from those needed for Hour 16.

#### 1. Temporal Specialization

Instead of a single "flat" output layer, we implement **Horizon Heads**. The model's bottleneck is split into four specialized branches:

* **Near-Term Head (Hours 1–4):** High-resolution, heavily weighted on $cpm25$ history and wind "momentum."
* **Mid-Term Head (Hours 5–8):** Focuses on the transition from momentum to meteorology.
* **Long-Term Head (Hours 9–12):** Heavily weighted on the diurnal cycle ($\sin/\cos$ time) and PBLH.
* **Horizon Head (Hours 13–16):** Fully atmospheric; focuses on large-scale weather drivers.

#### 2. Why This Wins

In a standard model, the gradients from the "easy" early hours can drown out the signals needed for the "hard" late hours. By using specialized heads, you ensure that the model has specific parameters dedicated to learning the **"Weather-Dominant"** physics of the final 16-hour forecast, preventing the late-horizon error accumulation that kills most submissions.

### Topic 6: The 3-Phase Loss Schedule

Standard training uses a single loss function (like MSE) for the whole run. However, your EDA Topic 4 and 11 showed that the **December Tail** is so extreme that a standard loss will under-fit the peaks.

#### 1. The "Stability-to-Sharpness" Transition

We implement a 3-phase schedule to guide the model's optimization:

* **Phase 1: MSE Stability (Epochs 1–20):** Train on **MSE** in log-normalized space. This teaches the model the "average" India-wide patterns.
* **Phase 2: Tail Sharpening (Epochs 21–40):** Switch to **Intensity-Weighted MAE**.
* **Formula:** $Loss = \text{mean}(W \times |y_{true} - y_{pred}|)$ where $W$ is proportional to the target concentration.
* **Goal:** This forces the model to care 5x more about getting a 400 µg/m³ peak right than a 10 µg/m³ clean day.


* **Phase 3: Smooth Softplus (Epochs 41–50):** Use a **Huber Loss** or **Log-Cosh**.
* **Goal:** This reduces noise in the final predictions, ensuring the output maps are smooth and physically consistent for the final leaderboard submission.

### Topic 7: Recurrent Neural Operator (RNO) Training

Your EDA (Topic 12) identified a "Persistence Plateau" after 10 hours. Most models suffer from **Teacher Forcing Bias**: during training, they always see the "perfect" past ground truth, but during inference, they must rely on their own (slightly imperfect) previous predictions. This causes errors to snowball exponentially.

#### 1. Recursive Rollout

The **Recurrent Neural Operator (RNO)** addresses this by training the model in a "recursive loop."

* **The Process:** During training, instead of just predicting all 16 hours at once, you feed the prediction for $t+1$ back into the model to help predict $t+2$.
* **The "Noise Injection" Trick:** We add a tiny amount of Gaussian noise to the model's own predictions during this loop. This teaches the model to be **stable** and "self-correcting" when it encounters the slight inaccuracies it will inevitably produce during the 16-hour forecast on the leaderboard.

#### 2. Why This Wins

Your Section 25 showed that persistence error is flat at the end. An RNO training strategy changes the error growth from **exponential** to **linear**. It ensures that your Hour 16 prediction is as physically grounded as your Hour 1, specifically addressing the "Blind Phase" ($t=10$ to $t=25$) where the ground-truth $cpm25$ is missing.

### Topic 8: Test-Time Augmentation (TTA) & Ensembling

This is the "Last Mile" of the competition. Once your Tucker-FrNO is trained, these post-processing techniques are what shave the final $1.0 - 1.5$ points off your RMSE to secure the Rank-1 position.

#### 1. Spatial Symmetry TTA

The laws of atmospheric physics are (mostly) spatially invariant. A dust storm moving East-to-West follows the same fluid equations as one moving West-to-East.

* **The Implementation:** For every test sample:
1. Predict the original grid.
2. Flip the grid horizontally, predict, and flip back.
3. Flip the grid vertically, predict, and flip back.
4. **Average** these three predictions.


* **The Result:** This "Self-Ensembling" cancels out the spatial bias of the model and acts as a powerful denoiser for the final `preds.npy`.

#### 2. Seed Ensembling

Because your dataset is small (2,932 hours), the model is sensitive to the "Random Initialization" of its weights.

* **The Implementation:** Train the exact same Tucker-FrNO architecture **three times** with different random seeds.
* **The Result:** Your EDA Section 29 showed that 90% of the variance is in 8 modes. Different seeds will capture different sub-modes. Averaging their outputs reduces the "variance" of your error, which is the most effective way to lower a high-stakes RMSE score.
